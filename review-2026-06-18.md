# Review: after-economy

## Verdict
Accept-with-major-revisions, leaning borderline. The scholarship is unusually clean — I could not find a fabricated or garbled citation, and every headline decimal in the prose traces exactly to `results.json` — and the central taxonomic move (unbundling cost/price/money/market/exchange into separable phenomena, each with a sharp exemplar) is a genuine contribution. But the paper's one piece of quantitative "rigor," the §11 allocation model, is a dressed-up tautology that proves nothing it didn't assume, and it sits in unacknowledged contradiction with the Hayek/Mises argument the paper endorses in §9. The single most serious problem: the model is sold as making the thesis "auditable" when nothing in it could have come out otherwise.

## Claim grounding
- **"a speculative markup on finance raises its budget share from 0.02 to 0.26 and costs 8.08 percent of system viability"** (Abstract) → *supported by the simulation* (`finance_share_optimum` 0.0213, `finance_share` 0.2628, `viability_loss_pct` 8.0812), but *overstated in status*. These are not measurements; the next clause ("They measure no actual economy") is correct and should govern the abstract, where the number currently reads as an empirical finding.
- **"The faithful price coordinator reproduces that allocation exactly: its divergence from the optimum is 0.0"** (§11) → *supported but trivial*. In the code, the faithful coordinator sets `p_i = 1`, which makes `water_fill` solve the *identical* optimization as the constraint coordinator. The "result" is `0.0` by construction, not by discovery. Presented as confirmation of the thesis, it is a restatement of the setup.
- **"undistorted prices reproduce the constraint-optimal allocation exactly, while a speculative markup ... costs 8.08 percent of viability"** → *circular as evidence for the real-world thesis*. The model defines the markup as a deviation from true marginal value and stipulates finance's true weight as low (0.3) by fiat (`W = [...0.30]`). That over-allocating to finance is costly is therefore baked into the premises; it cannot test whether real financial prices are distorted or whether real finance has low true contribution.
- **"A small allocation model makes the structural claim auditable"** (Abstract) / **"makes the structural claim auditable on a small model"** (§11) → *overstated*. The model *illustrates* the claim; it cannot *audit* it, because no parameterization could falsify "optimizing a distorted objective underperforms on the true objective." Nothing is at stake in the computation.
- **"Cells allocate energy, metabolites, membrane gradients, and repair capacity under viability constraints; they have costs and tradeoffs but no prices"** (Abstract/§3) → *supported* by Molenaar et al. and Scott et al. and properly hedged as analogy. Good.
- **"ATP ... lacks the institutional features of money: unit of account, social payment, legal settlement, durable store of value, property transfer, public price"** (§3) → *sound argument*, correctly bounded. Strong.
- **"The modern economy as a bounded macro-object is much later and depends on statistics, national accounting, and policy expertise, as Mitchell argues"** (§7) → *supported*; accurate to Mitchell (1998).
- **"Krippner's definition of financialization as a pattern in which profits accrue increasingly through financial channels"** (§9) → *supported*; faithful to Krippner.

## Argument
- **§9 vs. §11 — internal contradiction (the most damaging).** §9 endorses Hayek and Mises ("remains serious") — i.e., that no central allocator can possess the dispersed/tacit knowledge needed to compute the optimum. But §11's "constraint optimum" *is exactly that omniscient planner*: `water_fill` is handed the true weights `W` and maximizes total viability with full information. The model's whole force depends on a planner who knows what §9 says is unknowable. The paper never notices that its centerpiece presupposes what its own cited authorities deny.
- **§11 — the result is definitional, not demonstrative.** Faithful price ≡ the constraint optimization; distorted price ≡ a different objective. The gap between them exists by construction. Gap: a tautology is presented as auditable evidence.
- **Abstract / §1 / §12 — the headline claim deflates on inspection.** "After economy" is defined as "after the monetary-price form loses its *monopoly* over the interpretation of value," and the paper then lists numerous non-price allocation mechanisms that *already* operate (feeds, credit models, carbon accounts). On that definition the thesis is close to already-true, making the dramatic title cash out as a modest, near-definitional observation. The paper half-concedes this ("can survive only as a narrow claim") — but then the abstract and §11 reach for quantitative drama anyway.
- **§2–§4 — the analogy is both disclaimed and relied upon.** The biological cases are repeatedly called "dangerous," "analogy not identity," tempting "anthropomorphism" — yet they are also "the earliest level where the economy analogy becomes analytically useful" and do real argumentative work in the chain. An analogy hedged this hard cannot also carry the structure. Gap: the paper wants the rhetorical reach of the biology without the commitment.
- **§1 — the constraint→price chain is stipulated, not argued.** The ordering and the "economicity" threshold are asserted as the natural sequence; no argument rules out alternative orderings or shows the chain is more than a useful narrative spine.
- **Genuinely strong:** the §4 ball-catcher treatment of "transaction without exchange" (Gibson/Uexküll/active inference) is precise and earns its place; §6's analysis of wergild as "price without market" (commensuration/containment/hierarchy/sovereignty) is the cleanest section; §7's "the ledger precedes the market as a governing interface" is a real, defensible thesis well-grounded in Nissen/Schmandt-Besserat/Scott.

## Prose and insight
**Alive:** the ball-catcher passage; "the ledger precedes the market as a governing interface"; the wergild functions; the Banks "money as a sign of poverty" test and the coinage "oligarchic abundance"; "After price may arrive as more calculation while leaving domination untouched." These are doing real work.

**Dead:** the prose runs on long asyndetic noun-piles that substitute accumulation for argument — "Future income, volatility, risk, debt service, collateral, liquidity, state rescue"; "food, shelter, care, education, communication, transport, and cultural participation"; the abstract is a single 250-word firehose that buries its thesis. The audit itself records a "tricolon reflex" brought "from 86 to 61" — i.e., still 61 reflexive enumerations remain. The incantatory listing is the paper's verbal tic.

**Genuine contribution:** the unbundling — showing that cost, price, money, market, and exchange are separable, each instantiable without the others (cells, ball-catcher, gift, wergild, Mesopotamian ledger), and that "monetary civilization" mistakes one interface in the chain for the whole. That taxonomy is worth publishing. The §11 model does not add to it; it adds rigor-theater.

## Top fixes
1. **Resolve the §9↔§11 contradiction or cut the model's omniscient framing.** Either acknowledge that the "constraint optimum" assumes the full-information planner Hayek/Mises deny, and reframe §11 as a *purely illustrative* device that deliberately brackets the knowledge problem — or, far better, build the knowledge problem *into* the model (give the coordinators noisy/partial signals of `W`) so that faithful price can actually do informational work and the comparison stops being definitional. As written, §9 refutes §11.
2. **Demote the simulation from "auditable evidence" to "illustration," and strip its numbers from the abstract.** Replace "makes the structural claim auditable" and the "costs 8.08 percent" clause with language matching what the model is — a stylized geometry where a distorted objective underperforms the true one by construction. The honest disclaimer already in §11 should govern every mention of the model, including the abstract.
3. **State plainly that the §11 result is built in.** Add one sentence in §11 conceding that faithful-price divergence is `0.0` *because* the faithful coordinator solves the identical problem, and that finance's loss follows from stipulating its low true weight — so the model shows a mechanism's *direction*, not that any real economy exhibits it. This converts a hidden circularity into an owned limitation.
4. **Add reference entries for the named load-bearing thinkers without them:** Mises (calculation debate), Stafford Beer (Cybersyn), Girard, and a citable Banks source ("A Few Notes on the Culture"). A rigorous venue will flag named arguments with no citation.
5. **Tie the bibliography to the text or trim it.** Either cite the ~9 currently-unreferenced entries (Arrighi, Ostrom, Simon, Benkler, Varela, etc.) where they bear, or move them to a "further reading" note; a reference list should track the argument.
6. **Sharpen the headline claim against its own deflation.** Since "after economy" is defined as the loss of price's *monopoly* — a condition the paper shows is already partly here — say so up front and make the contribution the *taxonomy of what survives* and the *political-form question*, rather than letting the title promise a rupture the argument then narrows away.
7. **Break up the abstract and thin the noun-piles.** Split the 250-word abstract into 3–4 sentences that lead with the unbundling thesis; convert the worst remaining enumerations (the §9 finance list, the §10 necessities list) into claims with a verb. The audit's own count shows the reflex is only half-tamed.
8. **Reconcile the Mauss and Bourdieu citation conventions** (1925 journal vs. PUF book; 1972 original vs. 1977 translation) with the original-language-year style used elsewhere, for internal consistency.
